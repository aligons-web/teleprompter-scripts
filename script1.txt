Statistically speaking, 75% of US adults report regularly struggling to stay focused, with three-quarters citing issues like stress, lack of sleep, or digital device distractions. Furthermore, nearly 1 in 10 young adults (ages 18–39) suffer from diagnosed cognitive disabilities that severely impact their ability to concentrate, remember, or make decisions. [1, 2, 3, 4]
While a general "inability to stick to one idea" can stem from lifestyle fatigue or environment, it is also heavily tied to diagnosed medical conditions and broader psychological trends: [1, 2, 3, 4, 5]
Cognitive Difficulties: Between 2013 and 2023, self-reported cognitive difficulties nearly doubled among adults ages 18 to 39, rising from 5.1% to 9.7%. [1, 2]
ADHD Diagnoses: Roughly 6% of US adults (and about 1 in 9 children) currently live with ADHD, a neurodevelopmental condition directly characterized by severe struggles with executive function and sustained focus. [1, 2, 3]
The Shrinking Attention Span: The average time spent focusing on a single task on a screen has plummeted to roughly 40 seconds, a decrease primarily driven by the design of modern digital environments and social media. [1, 2]
Goal Achievement Rates: Studies reveal that 92% of people fail to achieve their goals, often due to the difficulty of maintaining focus on abstract, long-term plans over time. [1, 2]

SLIDE 10 
--- SECTION 3 CLARIFY YOUR CALLING --- C IN FOCUS
We all have the same challenges: time management, focus, and far too many things on our plate. The skill we're missing is knowing what should be done — and what should NOT be done.
I'll be transparent. I used to pride myself on how much I could multitask. Years ago I heard Joyce Meyer and Joel Osteen talk about doing too many things and not being grounded or focused. At the time I quietly rebuked it. My thought was, “They don't know what they're talking about — they don't have to work through what I face as a Black man.”
Looking back now, I wonder how much further, faster I would have gotten had I embraced focus sooner instead of trying to do everything at once.
So I want you to honestly reflect:
Is multitasking actually moving you forward, or quietly slowing your progress?
How much of your clarity is being stolen by simply doing too many things?
What would happen if you concentrated your effort on fewer things with greater intensity?
Study highly successful people and you'll find a pattern: they did not become successful with a constantly divided mind. They committed to one main thing long enough to see it grow.
Your future success is directly linked to your ability to focus on one main thing and push it forward with consistency.
